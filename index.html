Geometric Representation

Geometric Algebra in 4D

Space-time Algebra (STA)

We define the basis vectors as: $$\gamma_0 ^2 = 1, \gamma_k ^2 = -1, \gamma _ \mu \cdot \gamma _ \nu = 0 , \, (\mu \neq \nu) $$

We get a 16 terms algebra:
1 scalar, 4 vectors ($\gamma_\mu$), 6 bivectors ($\gamma_\mu \wedge \gamma_\nu$), 4 trivectors ($I \gamma_\mu$), 1 pseudoscalar $I \equiv \gamma_0\gamma_1\gamma_2\gamma_3$

Space-time split
We recover 'normal' observer dependent 3D vector by defining $$\vec e_i = \vec \sigma _ i \equiv \gamma_{i0} = \gamma_i \wedge \gamma _0$$ and recover: $$\vec \sigma_1 \times \vec \sigma_2 = -I \vec \sigma_1 \wedge \vec \sigma_2 = \vec \sigma_3$$
Space-time split examples

  • $x^2 = x \gamma_0 \gamma_0 x = [ct+ \vec x][ct - \vec x] = (ct)^2 - |\vec x|^2$
  • $\Box = \gamma _0 (\partial_0 + \vec \nabla)$

Maxwell Equations

The Faraday Bivector

We define the faraday bivector: \begin{align} F = E_1 \gamma_{10} + E_2 \gamma_{20} + E_3 \gamma_{30} + \\ B_1 \gamma_{32} + B_2 \gamma_{13} + B_3 \gamma_{21} = \vec E + I \vec B \end{align}

Maxwell Equations in free space

$$\Box F = 0 $$

  • Scalar: $\vec \nabla \cdot \vec E = 0$
  • Relative vector: $\partial _t \vec E + I \vec \nabla \wedge \vec B = 0$
  • Relative bivector: $I \partial _t \vec B + \vec \nabla \wedge \vec E = 0$
  • Pseudoscalar: $I \vec \nabla \cdot \vec B = 0$

Maxwell Equations

$$\Box F = j$$ where $j =(\rho + \vec j) \gamma_0 $

  • Scalar: $\vec \nabla \cdot \vec E = \rho$
  • Relative vector: $\partial _t \vec E + I \vec \nabla \wedge \vec B = -\vec j$

Charge Conservation
\begin{align} \Box ^2 F = \Box j \\ = \Box \cdot j + \Box \wedge j \end{align}

Separating the scalar part we get: $$\Box \cdot j = 0 = \partial_t \rho + \vec \nabla \cdot \vec j = 0 $$

Scalar Invariants

We take the scalar and pseudoscalar part of $F^2$: $$F^2=(\vec E+I \vec B)^2= \vec E^2 - \vec B^2 + 2I(\vec E \cdot \vec B)$$

Conclusion

  • Usual 3-vectors are 'relative' spacetime bivectors
  • 4D vector represented in 3D by a scalar + a relative vector
  • Maxwell equation in vacuum $\Box F =0$
    • With sources $\Box F = j$
  • Invariants are part of $F^2$
  • $i$ is a pseudo-scalar oriented volume element
The principal argument for the adoption of geometric algebra is that it provides a single, simple mathematical framework which eliminates the need for the plethora of diverse mathematical descriptions and techniques that it would otherwise be necessary to learn.
